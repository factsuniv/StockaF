The Power of Compound Interest

What is Compound Interest?

Compound interest is interest calculated on the initial principal and also on the accumulated interest from previous periods. Think of it as "interest on interest" - which makes your money grow exponentially rather than linearly.

The Formula

A = P(1 + r)^t

Where:

  • A = Final amount
  • P = Principal (initial investment)
  • r = Interest rate (decimal)
  • t = Time (years)

Example: $10,000 at 8% for 30 Years

Starting with $10,000 and earning 8% annually, after 30 years your investment would grow to approximately $100,627 - over 10 times your initial investment!

The Rule of 72

The Rule of 72 is a simplified way to determine how long an investment will take to double at a given annual rate of return.

Formula: 72 ÷ Annual Rate = Years to Double

  • At 6% interest, money doubles in approximately 12 years
  • At 8% interest, money doubles in approximately 9 years
  • At 12% interest, money doubles in approximately 6 years

Common Stock Chart Patterns

Head and Shoulders

A bearish reversal pattern resembling a head with two shoulders. The pattern is complete when the price breaks below the neckline after forming the right shoulder, often signaling a trend reversal from bullish to bearish.

Double Bottom (W Pattern)

A bullish reversal pattern forming after a downtrend. The price drops to a support level twice, forming a "W" shape. When the price breaks above the middle peak between the two bottoms, it signals a potential uptrend.

Symmetrical Triangle

A consolidation pattern where price forms lower highs and higher lows, creating converging trend lines that form a triangle. The breakout direction often indicates the next trend direction. Volume typically decreases during the pattern formation.

What is the Stock Market?

The stock market is a collection of exchanges where shares of publicly traded companies are bought and sold. When you buy a stock, you're purchasing a small piece of ownership in that company.

The price of these stocks fluctuates based on supply and demand, which is influenced by countless factors including company performance, economic conditions, and investor sentiment.

Key Market Participants

Investors

Individuals or institutions who buy securities with the intention of holding them for longer periods, typically focusing on fundamental value and long-term growth.

Traders

Individuals or institutions who buy and sell securities with the intention of profiting from short-term price movements, often using technical analysis and market trends.

Brokers

Firms or individuals who execute buy and sell orders on behalf of investors and traders, charging commissions or fees for their services. Modern discount brokers have significantly reduced these fees.

Market Makers

Financial institutions that provide liquidity to markets by standing ready to buy and sell securities at quoted prices, profiting from the bid-ask spread.

Major Stock Market Indexes

S&P 500

Tracks 500 large-cap U.S. companies across various industries, weighted by market capitalization.

Dow Jones Industrial Average

Tracks 30 large, publicly-owned U.S. companies, weighted by share price.

Nasdaq Composite

Tracks over 3,000 companies listed on the Nasdaq exchange, with a heavy weight toward technology.

The Power of Compound Interest

What is Compound Interest?

Compound interest is often called the "eighth wonder of the world" for good reason. It's the interest you earn on both your original money and on the interest you keep accumulating.

Unlike simple interest, which is calculated only on the principal amount, compound interest is calculated on the principal plus all the interest that has been accumulated over time. This creates an exponential growth effect that can dramatically increase your wealth over long periods.

The Rule of 72

The Rule of 72 is a simple formula to determine how long an investment will take to double at a given annual rate of return.

Years to Double = 72 ÷ Annual Interest Rate (%)

For example, if you're earning 8% annually, your investment will double in approximately 9 years (72 ÷ 8 = 9).

Understanding Chart Patterns

Chart patterns are specific formations that appear on price charts. These patterns can help traders predict future price movements based on historical patterns of trader behavior.

While no pattern guarantees a particular outcome, recognizing these patterns can give you an edge in making trading decisions. Chart patterns typically fall into two categories: continuation patterns and reversal patterns.

Interactive Chart Patterns

Click on each pattern to see how they form and what they might indicate about future price movements.

Head and Shoulders

A bearish reversal pattern signaling a potential trend change from bullish to bearish. The pattern completes when price breaks below the neckline after forming the right shoulder.

Double Bottom (W Pattern)

A bullish reversal pattern indicating a potential trend change from bearish to bullish. The pattern completes when price breaks above the middle peak between the two bottoms, often signaling the start of an uptrend.

Symmetrical Triangle

A consolidation pattern where price forms lower highs and higher lows, creating converging trend lines. The breakout direction often indicates the next trend direction, with increasing volume confirming the breakout.

Bull Flag

A continuation pattern that forms after a strong price move (the flagpole), followed by a consolidation period (the flag). The pattern suggests the previous trend will continue once price breaks out of the flag in the direction of the initial trend.

Trading with Chart Patterns

To effectively use chart patterns in your trading:

  • Look for patterns on multiple timeframes to confirm signals
  • Pay attention to volume - increasing volume often confirms breakouts
  • Wait for confirmation of the pattern completion before trading
  • Use pattern targets to set realistic profit objectives
  • Always employ proper risk management with stop losses
